Phase Three

Captain America: Civil War (2016)

Much like the previous Captain America film, The Winter Soldier, Civil War was basically another mini-Avengers movie. It was based on the comic event of the same name where the US government (the United Nations in the movie) requires superheroes to become registered and their activity monitored as a result of the culmination of events at the start of this film and previous ones which ended with the loss of civilian life.

The Avengers is split down the middle between a pro-registration and anti-registration side who end up coming to blows. Unknowingly to them, this is a plot being masterminded by Baron Zemo, a rogue ex-millitary man whose family died in Sokovia during Ultron's attack in Avengers: Age of Ultron. His character here differs greatly from the comics where he is a member of Hydra working under the Red Skull.

Recommended reading: Marvel: Civil War

Thor: Ragnarok (2017)

Despite being a Thor movie, a lot of the source material used was the 2006 Planet Hulk comic where Hulk is banished to space for causing too much destruction on earth, but ends up crash landing on an alien planet where he is captured and forced to fight others as a gladiator, with one of his opponents predictably being Thor, who also happens to have been stranded on the same planet after a battle with the godess of death, Hela. Marvel don't actually own the rights to create a Hulk film (those rights belong to Universal), but the can use the character so creating what's basically a "buddy" movie with Thor is basically the next best thing.

Recommended reading: Planet Hulk

Black Panther (2018)

The character of Black Panther was previosulty introduced in Captain America: Civil War and the events of that lead into this origin story of T'Challa taking over the mantle of King of Wakanda, from his deceased father.

A new comic series, written by Ta-Nehisi Coates and drawn by Brian Stelfreeze, was launched in August 2016 a year and a half before the movie and both shared a very similar overall art style, character design and environmental design. One major influence was the design of the movie suit adapting the kinetic absorption powers that were introduced in that comic.

Recommended reading: Black Panther: A Nation Under Our Feet

Avengers: Infinity War (2018)

Part one of the final chapter of the whole Infinity Saga, the finale of the first three MCU phases. Despite the title, this didn’t have much, if any, similarities with the Infinity War comic. Instead it drew most inspiration from two major titles:

  • The two-issue Thanos Quest mini-series by Jim Starlin which chronicles Thanos as he hunts down all six Infinity Stones to allow him to achieve the power of a god.
  • The six-issue Infinity Gauntlet, also by Jim Starlin, where Thanos uses the Infinity Stones to wipe out 50% of all living organisms in the universe, and the surviving heroes attempts to reverse this.

Thanos’ motivation for doing so here is slightly different. In the comics he is infatuated with the physical manifestation of Mistress Death and does it as a means to impress her and seek her affection. However, this is completely changed for the movie with it being to control the threat of the overpopulation of the universe.

Given the sheer scale of the film, from the amount of characters, locations and plot elements, this has probably the most references and easter eggs from the comics than any other in the series. The Hulk crashing through the roof of Dr Strange’s Sanctum is a recreation of the same scene from Infinity Gauntlet, but with Silver Surfer instead.
